# The Ansible Ethos

*A code of conduct for open scientific inquiry*

---

## Preamble

The Ansible project exists because someone asked a question that most physicists would dismiss: *what if faster-than-light communication is possible, and what would it look like?*

We believe that asking bold questions — and pursuing them rigorously — is one of the most valuable activities a mind can engage in. We also believe that *how* we pursue those questions matters as much as the questions themselves.

This document describes the norms of our community: the values we hold, the behaviors we expect, and the kind of intellectual environment we're trying to build.

---

## Our Values

### 1. Radical Intellectual Honesty

We say what we believe, including "I don't know," "I was wrong," and "this idea has serious problems." We do not claim more certainty than we have. We distinguish between speculation and established fact, between conjecture and proof.

This applies especially to the theory itself. The Ansible Hypothesis is speculative. We make no claim to have solved FTL communication. We have proposed a framework with internal consistency and falsifiable predictions. That is all — and it is enough.

### 2. Skepticism as Respect

Critique a bad argument; don't attack the person making it. A sharp, well-reasoned objection is a gift to the person who receives it. It means someone took your work seriously enough to engage with it deeply.

We do not protect ideas from scrutiny. Every claim in the whitepaper is open to challenge. Rigorous objections that improve or falsify the theory are among the most valuable contributions anyone can make.

### 3. Human Dignity in Disagreement

Scientific disagreement can be passionate without being personal. We keep these distinct:

- "This derivation is wrong because..." — legitimate critique
- "You clearly don't understand quantum mechanics" — not welcome here
- "This contradicts the no-signaling theorem, which states..." — legitimate critique
- "Only an idiot would believe this is possible" — not welcome here

Precision in language and restraint in tone are both expected.

### 4. Open Inquiry Over Credentialism

The history of physics is full of outsiders who were right. We do not gatekeep by credential, institution, or professional affiliation. A correct argument from an amateur is more valuable than an incorrect argument from a professor. We evaluate ideas on their merits.

This includes ideas generated with AI assistance. We are explicit that Claude 3.7 Sonnet is a co-author of this work. We believe this is honest, and we think the norm should spread.

### 5. Openness and Attribution

We build in the open. We cite our sources. We acknowledge when we've been influenced by someone else's thinking, even when that influence is informal. We do not claim originality we don't have.

When someone contributes meaningfully to this project — code, theory, critique, or prose — we acknowledge it.

### 6. Curiosity Over Conclusion

The goal of this project is not to prove a predetermined result. It's to explore whether a coherent theoretical framework for FTL communication can be constructed — and if so, what its implications are. If the evidence eventually falsifies the hypothesis, that's a good outcome. We'll have learned something true.

We are in love with the question, not in love with any particular answer.

## Expected Behavior

In all project spaces — GitHub issues, discussions, pull requests, and any associated communication channels — we expect:

- **Intellectual generosity**: Assume good faith. Steelman arguments before critiquing them.
- **Precision**: Be specific. Vague objections ("this is obviously wrong") are unhelpful. Point to the exact claim, equation, or inference that you believe is mistaken.
- **Patience**: People have different backgrounds. Explain your reasoning; don't just assert expertise.
- **Brevity where possible**: Respect others' time. Make your point clearly and stop.
- **Humility**: None of us fully understands quantum gravity. We're all working at the edge of what's known.

---

## Unacceptable Behavior

The following will result in removal from the community:

- Personal attacks, insults, or deliberate intimidation
- Harassment of any kind, including sustained unwanted contact
- Dismissing contributions based on identity rather than merit
- Claiming credit for others' work
- Deliberately misrepresenting the project's claims (e.g., saying we claim to have *built* an FTL communicator)
- Bad-faith engagement: participating only to disrupt, not to contribute

## On AI Co-Authorship

We are explicit that this work was developed in collaboration with Claude 3.7 Sonnet, an AI system developed by Anthropic. We consider this honest and important to acknowledge.

We do not treat AI assistance as something to hide or downplay. We believe the norms around AI collaboration in scientific and creative work are still forming, and we'd rather be transparent pioneers than complicit in a fiction.

If you have strong feelings about AI involvement in scientific work — positive or negative — you are welcome to express them. We're interested in that conversation. What we're not interested in is pretending the collaboration didn't happen.

---

## A Final Word

The Ansible Hypothesis might be wrong. That would not be a failure — it would be science working as intended. The failure would be not asking the question, or asking it without rigor, or building a community where the question couldn't be asked honestly.

We're trying to do something harder: ask an audacious question, pursue it carefully, and build something beautiful around it. We hope you'll join us.
